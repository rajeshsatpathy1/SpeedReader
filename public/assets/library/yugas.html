Wheel of Ages: Aethel Vane

Prologue: The Wheel of Yugas

Before time learned to count itself, before the first word was spoken into void, there existed the Beginning—the Primordial God whose breath became reality itself.

In the first moment of consciousness, the Beginning understood its own loneliness. So it created others like itself—Primordial Gods, vast and infinite, each a different aspect of existence. These beings, in turn, became creators themselves. They shaped gods to govern their designs, humans to experience them, and demons to test them. Creation sprawled outward, layer upon layer, hierarchy upon hierarchy, until it grew so vast across time and space that no single mind could comprehend its scope. Beginning and end became meaningless concepts in the face of infinite complexity.

But the Beginning made a choice that set it apart from all other Primordial Gods: it decided to participate in what had been created. While its siblings and children remained distant observers—architects admiring their work from beyond—the Beginning chose to descend. To be born. To live and die as part of creation rather than apart from it.

This was how the Yugas began—the cyclical sagas of existence itself.

Across infinite multiverses, the pattern repeats: the Beginning incarnates into mortal form, its presence igniting cosmic tensions that erupt into war between gods and demons, and at the conclusion, reality is remade. Four ages compose each Yuga: the golden age of truth, the age of ceremony, the age of doubt, and finally the age of darkness. At the end of every age, the Beginning returns in flesh, orchestrates a cataclysmic war, and the wheel turns to the next and anew.

Some universes welcome this cosmic reset. Others do not.

In countless realities, civilizations have learned to dread the incarnation. They see not renewal but endless suffering—worlds destroyed, lives erased, histories rendered meaningless by the whim of divine conflict. The Beginning cares nothing for mortal attachment. To the Primordial God, creation and destruction are merely the inhale and exhale of existence.

The Immortal Sage was born in one such universe that had learned to hate the wheel.

He watched his world burn in the war between gods and demons—a war sparked by the Beginning's incarnation, a war that treated humanity as kindling. While others prayed for salvation, the Sage studied the fundamental architecture of reality. While others accepted the Yuga as inevitable, he sought to do the impossible: to spit in the face of the Primordial God and stop the cycle forever.

His solution was radical. He could not kill the Beginning—nothing could. But he could starve it of its cyclical pleasures, delay its incarnation, make it powerless in this world.

The Sage shattered the connection between his world and the realm of Prana, the source of all magic. He created the Separation—a world of stagnation, severing divine power from mortal reality. Gods and demons alike were banished to the other side of an impenetrable barrier. Without magic, without divine interference, without the flow of Prana, the world would remain frozen in time—unable to grow, unable to evolve, unable to reach the conditions necessary for the Beginning's incarnation to trigger the next Yuga.

For three thousand years, it worked. Magic became myth. The incarnations ceased. Humanity survived in a dimmed world, but it survived.

Yet the Separation was always temporary. Like a dam holding back an infinite ocean, it could only delay the inevitable. Cracks formed. Prana began to seep through. And those who remembered the old ways—scavengers of forbidden knowledge, collectors of divine refuse—began to sense what was coming.

The Beginning would return. Not as a god descending from heaven, but as it always did: born mortal, fragile, unaware.

This is the story of one such incarnation in a dying industrial world called Dhūma—a realm that had forgotten gods, forgotten magic, forgotten even the stars themselves. In the capital city of Aethel Vane, where smoke choked the sky and ruins marked the landscape, the Beginning would wake as a sickly scavenger's son, knowing nothing of the cosmic war his very existence would ignite.

And this time, there were those who would try to use him before the wheels turned once again.

Scavenger's Son 

The smoke hit Rishi's lungs before his eyes opened.

He jerked awake in the cramped sleeping quarters behind the scrap shop, coughing into his sleeve until his ribs burned. Through the single grimy window, the city sprawled beneath a colorless sky—no sun, no stars, only the perpetual gray that had hung over Aethel Vane for as long as anyone could remember. Collapsed towers jutted from the industrial wasteland like broken teeth. The air tasted of rust and ash.

"Get up," his mother called from the shop. Her voice held no warmth, only the sharp efficiency of someone who had long ago discarded anything that didn't aid survival. "The haul from District Nine came in. Sort it before the morning patrol."

Rishi pushed himself upright, ignoring the familiar ache in his joints. At fifteen, he looked twelve—gaunt, hollow-cheeked, his skin the color of old paper. He'd been sick his entire life, though no one could name the illness. His mother, Maya Vosh, attributed it to weakness. Fragile, she called him. Useless. The city's polluted air should have killed him years ago, but he persisted, clinging to life with the same stubborn resilience that kept the scavengers digging through ruins for scraps of the old world.

He stumbled into the shop, where his mother was already sorting through twisted metal and broken machinery. Maya was sharp angles and efficiency, her hands moving through the debris with the precision of a surgeon. She spared him a glance—measuring, dismissing—then gestured to a pile of corroded gears.

"Start there. And don't touch the electrical components. You'll ruin them."

Rishi knelt by the pile, his fingers trembling slightly as he separated salvageable pieces from waste. This was his life: sorting through the corpse of a world that had died before he was born, under the cold supervision of a mother who seemed to resent his existence. He told himself it didn't matter. Survival mattered. Nothing else.

But sometimes, late at night, he would climb to the roof and stare at the empty sky, feeling an inexplicable hunger gnaw at him—as if something vital had been stolen, as if the colorless void above should contain something his bones remembered but his mind could not name.

***

The bell above the shop door chimed.

Maya's hands went still. In fifteen years, Rishi had never seen his mother freeze.

A woman stood in the doorway, and she was wrong in every possible way. Her clothes were pristine white in a city that stained everything gray within hours. Her skin was pale as bone, her eyes the color of fresh blood—an albino, though Rishi had only read about such people in salvaged books. She moved with liquid grace, utterly unbothered by the smoke and grime that choked everything around her.

"Maya Vosh?" Her voice was soft, almost kind. "My name is Yuki. I am the Recruiter of the Incarnated, one of the highest ranked of the Theological Military. I've come for the boy."

Maya's hand shot out, grabbing a crowbar from beneath the counter. "Get out."

The woman tilted her head, surprised. "I represent the Kindred. You know what that means."

The crowbar shook in Maya's grip. Rishi had never seen his mother afraid before. Fear looked wrong on her, like rust on polished steel.

"He's sick," Maya said, her voice cracking. "He's weak. He's no use to you!"

"We're not looking for strength." Yuki's red eyes shifted to Rishi, and something flickered in her expression—recognition? curiosity? A strange melancholy, almost longing, as if she saw something in the boy she'd never had herself. An orphan recognizing what family looked like, even a broken one. Then her professional mask returned. "We're looking for potential. And this one... he burns so bright I could see him from across the city."

Shadows moved in the doorway. Two figures stepped into view, silent and armed, their faces hidden behind cloth masks. Guards. Enforcers. She couldn't fight against them.

Maya's shoulders sagged, and the crowbar clattered to the floor.

"Three days," she whispered. "Please, give me at least three days with my boy."

Yuki considered this, her head tilting again like a curious bird. Something softened in her expression—that melancholy deepening. Finally, she nodded. "Three days. Then I return, and he comes with me willingly or otherwise. Prepare him for his new life." She turned to leave, then paused at the threshold. "The Citadel awaits, Rishi Vosh. Elder Rahu will be eager to meet you."

When they were gone, when the bell had stopped chiming and the shop fell silent once more, Maya collapsed against the counter. Her whole body shook.

"Maa?" Rishi's voice was small, squeaking with fear.

She looked at him then, really looked at him, and for the first time in his life he saw something other than cold efficiency in her eyes. Something that might have been grief. Or love. Or terror.

"I tried to save you," she whispered. "I tried so hard."

***

For three days, everything changed.

Maya fed him real food—not the thin gruel and stale bread that had been his sustenance, but meat and vegetables salvaged from the better districts. She took him to the roof at night, where the empty sky stretched overhead, and told him stories of his grandmother who had known the stars before they vanished. His grandmother had been there when Dhūma was already in stagnation, but she'd passed down stories from her own parents—memories of when the only star visible was the sun, before even that had dimmed to perpetual gray.

On the third night, as they sat amid the smoke and ruins, Maya finally spoke the truth.

"You were dying from the moment you were born," she said, her voice hollow. "Not from the air or the pollution. From what you are. Your grandmother warned me. She said you carried something ancient, something that would consume you if I let it wake."

Rishi stared at her. "What are you talking about?"

"Magic." The word sounded like blasphemy. "It still exists, Rishi. Beyond the Separation. Beyond this dead world. And it's in you, trying to surface, trying to burn through your fragile body. So I starved it. I kept you weak, kept you hungry. Every cruel word, every punishment, every cold dismissal—I did it to keep you alive."

Understanding crashed through him like falling glass, but with it came something unexpected—relief. He'd always thought he was just a burden, a weight dragging her down. But this... "You were protecting me."

"I was killing you slowly so you wouldn't die quickly. I know I was harsh. I didn't want you to go out and be found, to become strong and become a danger to yourself." Maya's fingers trembled as she continued. "We hid here, in this symbol of collapse, because it was the last place anyone would look for something divine. Your father..." Her voice cracked. "He left us. You weren't born of him, Rishi. You were born of Divine Providence. When he realized what you were—or thought I had cheated—he couldn't stay. Housing an incarnated was worse than betrayal to him. So he separated himself from us, and I let him go."

Rishi felt his world tilt. "I don't have a father?"

"You have something more terrifying than a father." Maya's hand moved to the pendant around Rishi's neck—the small silver disk etched with symbols that seemed to shift in the dim light. He'd worn it for as long as he could remember, a weight so familiar he barely noticed it anymore. "This was your grandmother's. I always told you it would protect you. And it has—it's kept your power dormant, kept you weak enough to survive. I've heard of it being used to imprison kings." Her fingers trembled as she touched it. "I hoped it could protect you a little bit longer. But now..." She let her hand fall. "Now you need to wake up. And I can't stop that anymore."

"I don't understand," Rishi said. "What am I?"

"I don't know." For the first time, Maya touched him—her hand on his cheek, gentle and devastating. "But the Kindred do. They hunt children like you. Incarnates, they call them. Children touched by gods. And Rahu..." Her voice hardened. "Rahu collects them like tools."

"Then why let me go?"

"Because I can't stop them. And because..." She pulled him close, awkward and desperate, the embrace of someone who had forgotten how to show affection. "Because maybe you were never mine to keep. You belong to something bigger than this dying city. Bigger than me. Maybe... maybe I was wrong about everything. Maybe you were meant for more than hiding."

In her own twisted way, she had been protecting Rishi all along. And now, she was setting him free.

***

When Yuki returned on the fourth day, Rishi was ready. The pendant lay warm against his chest, its familiar weight now carrying new meaning—not just protection, but his mother's love, and the faith she'd placed in his grandmother's gift.

Maya stood in the doorway of the shop, her face a mask once more, but her eyes... her eyes told him everything.

I love you. I'm sorry. I should have told you when you were here with me. Survive.

Rishi followed Yuki into the smoke, into the waiting portal that would take him to the Citadel, into a destiny he couldn't begin to comprehend. Behind him, Maya Vosh stood alone in a shop full of broken things, and for the first time in fifteen years, she allowed herself to weep.

She regretted not telling him. Not saying the words. But perhaps he'd known anyway.

Beautiful Chains

The portal felt like dying.

Rishi's body dissolved into sensation—heat and cold and pressure and absence—before reforming on the other side. He collapsed onto smooth white stone, gasping, his lungs suddenly, impossibly full of clean air. No smoke. No ash. His chest expanded properly for the first time in his life.

"Breathe," Yuki said from somewhere above him. "The shock passes."

When his vision cleared, Rishi understood why they called it the Citadel.

Impossible architecture stretched in every direction—towers of white stone and crystal that defied geometry, bridges that hung without support, gardens where plants grew in colors he had no names for. And everywhere, everywhere, there was light. Not the gray pallor of home but genuine sunlight, warm and golden, streaming through windows that shouldn't exist.

But more than the light, more than the beauty, Rishi felt something else. A presence in the air, thick and nourishing like water after a lifetime of thirst. It filled his lungs, soaked into his skin, fed something deep in his core that had been screaming silently his entire life.

The hunger vanished. The ache in his bones disappeared. For the first time since birth, Rishi felt whole.

"Prana," Yuki explained, watching him with those unsettling red eyes. "The breath of creation. Magic in its purest form. The Separation keeps it locked away from your world, but here..." She gestured at the luminous air. "Here, it flows freely."

"Is that why I don't feel sick anymore?" Strangely, Rishi felt more confident too, just breathing in that air. His voice was steadier, his thoughts clearer.

"Because you were starving." She offered her hand to help him stand. "Come. Let me show you the Citadel before you meet Elder Rahu."

***

They walked through corridors of polished stone, past gardens where other children trained—some moving with inhuman grace, others manipulating elements with casual gestures. All of them looked healthy, vibrant, alive in a way Rishi had never seen before.

"These are the incarnates," Yuki explained. "Children touched by divinity, like you. The Kindred gathers them from across the dying world, brings them here where they can thrive."

"It seems like a paradise," Rishi said, watching a boy his age conjure fire between his palms.

"Elder Rahu takes good care of his people." There was something odd in Yuki's tone, but Rishi couldn't place it. "You'll see."

They passed through an archway into a vast chamber, and Rishi stopped breathing.

The council chamber was a cathedral of carved and polished rock, its ceiling lost in shadow far above. But what drew his eye—what froze him in place—were the pillars.

Children. Dozens of them, each suspended in crystalline cylinders filled with viscous liquid that bent light strangely, its refractive index making the bodies shimmer and distort. They floated peacefully, eyes closed, perfectly preserved. All of them were healthy—full cheeks, strong limbs, no signs of the malnutrition or sickness that plagued the dying world. Even in stasis, they looked more alive than anyone Rishi had ever known.

"What..." His voice failed him.

"The Preserved," Yuki said quietly. "Children whose incarnations were too powerful, too dangerous to awaken fully. Elder Rahu keeps them here, safe, until a solution can be found." She moved forward, her reflection rippling in the cylinders. "He takes care of them. Keeps them healthy, free from pain. It's... a kindness, in its way."

Rishi wanted to believe her. The children did look peaceful. Well-fed. Protected.

But something about the scene made his grandmother's pendant grow warm against his chest.

Guards stood at intervals throughout the chamber—men and women in white armor, all of them radiating that same vitality. Rahu's people were clearly well-provided for. Whatever else the Kindred might be, they weren't neglecting their own.

"This way," Yuki said, leading him toward the raised dais at the chamber's center. "Elder Rahu awaits."

***

Rishi climbed the steps, his bare feet silent on polished stone, and stopped when he saw what sat upon the throne.

A head. Just a head.

It floated three feet above an ornate bronze pedestal, flesh preserved but clearly ancient, withered lips pulled back from yellowed teeth. Two eyes—still somehow alive—tracked Rishi's entrance with predatory focus. When it spoke, the voice emerged from somewhere deeper than the throat, resonating in Rishi's chest like a second heartbeat.

"So," Elder Rahu said. "Another incarnate comes to us. Welcome, Rishi Vosh, to your new home."

Rishi's grandmother's pendant suddenly burned against his chest—not painfully, but warming, maybe warning. His hand moved reflexively to clutch it through his shirt.

Rahu's eyes narrowed. "Interesting. You carry a ward. An old one, too—I can taste its age from here. Did your mother think she could hide you from destiny with trinkets and starvation?"

"I don't understand what's happening."

"Of course you don't. You're newly awakened, barely aware of what you are." The floating head drifted closer, circling Rishi like a shark. "But I understand. I've been gathering children like you for centuries, helping them reach their potential. You're stronger than most—I can sense it. The power sleeping in you is... considerable."

Yuki stepped forward. "Elder, perhaps Rishi could join the other incarnates? Train with them, learn to control his abilities? He's come so far already—"

"Silence." Rahu's tone was sharp, and Yuki flinched as if struck. In all her years serving the Kindred, the Elder had never spoken to her that way. Something was different about this boy. Something that unsettled even Rahu. "This one requires... special attention. Guards!"

Two armored figures moved forward, producing heavy manacles from their belts—iron bands inscribed with glowing symbols.

"What are you doing?" Rishi backed away, but Yuki caught his shoulder, holding him steady. Her grip was apologetic but firm.

"Just a precaution," Rahu said smoothly. "That ward you carry is suppressing your power, but here, saturated in Prana, it will fail eventually. When it does, the surge could destroy you—and everyone around you. These bindings will help channel the energy safely."

"That's never been necessary before," Yuki said, her voice uncertain. "The other children—"

"Were not like him." Rahu's eyes never left Rishi. "Trust me, Recruiter. I know what I'm doing. This makes things easier for everyone."

The guards clamped the manacles around Rishi's wrists. The symbols flared bright, and he gasped as something cold and heavy settled over him—not painful, but constraining, like invisible hands pressing down on his shoulders.

"There," Rahu said with satisfaction. "Now you won't accidentally immolate yourself. Or us." He drifted back toward his pedestal. "Take him to the eastern tower. Let him rest, acclimate to the Prana. In three days, we'll begin his training properly."

As the guards led Rishi away, he looked back at Yuki. She stood alone in the chamber, surrounded by the sleeping children in their cylinders, and her expression was troubled.

Something was wrong here. Rishi could feel it, even through the strange numbness of the manacles.

But he was too tired, too overwhelmed to resist. He let himself be guided through corridors and up spiral staircases, into a chamber with a bed softer than anything he'd ever known, and when they left him alone, he collapsed onto it and stared at the ceiling.

The pendant still lay warm against his chest. A small comfort. A reminder that his mother had tried to protect him.

He just wished he understood from what.

***

Three days passed in a strange blur.

Rishi remained in his chamber, visited only by silent servants who brought food—real food, more than he'd eaten in his entire life combined. The manacles stayed on his wrists, their weight a constant reminder that he was, despite all the luxury, still a prisoner.

But the Prana... the Prana changed him.

Even through the bindings, even with the pendant suppressing his power, he could feel it working on his body. His joints stopped aching. His breathing came easier. When he looked in the polished bronze mirror, he saw color returning to his cheeks, his frame filling out slightly. He was still thin, still weak—but less so. Every breath was healing him.

And with the healing came dreams.

Terrible, beautiful dreams of things that made no sense: stars being named into existence, wars between beings of pure light, a wheel turning through infinite darkness. He dreamed of dying and being born again, over and over, each life a different face but the same essential loneliness.

He dreamed of a brilliant man with sad eyes who looked at the heavens and whispered, "No more."

On the third night, Yuki came to visit.

She entered without knocking, her clothes stained with something dark—ash? Blood? Why hadn’t she used her artifact?—and her face drawn with exhaustion and something else. Fear. Determination.

"We need to talk," she said, locking the door behind her. "And we don't have much time."

Rishi sat up. "What's wrong?"

"Everything." Yuki moved to the window, checking the courtyard below before turning back to him. "I need you to listen carefully, Rishi. Elder Rahu is not what you think. The Kindred is not what I thought."

"What do you mean?"

"Those children in the cylinders—the Preserved." Her voice shook. "I've been investigating. They're not in stasis because they're too dangerous. They're dead, Rishi. Or as good as. Rahu drained them, fed on their divine essence to sustain himself and the Citadel."

The pendant flared hot against Rishi's chest. "No. They looked healthy—"

"Because he keeps the bodies preserved. But their souls, their power..." Yuki's hands clenched into fists. "He killed them. Seventeen children over the centuries, all of them trusting him to protect them. And now he's planning to use you—"

The door exploded inward.

Rahu floated into the chamber, flanked by a dozen guards, his ancient face twisted with rage. "I knew you'd turn traitor eventually, Yuki. You always were too soft for this work."

Yuki's hand moved to her blade, but the guards were already moving, surrounding her. She didn't draw—couldn't, not against so many.

"You lied to me," she said, her voice hard. "You told me we were saving them."

"I am saving them." Rahu's attention shifted to Rishi. "I'm saving all of creation. The Separation is failing—can't you feel it? The Sage's work was always temporary. When it falls completely, magic will flood back into every world at once. Chaos. Destruction. The Yuga will turn, and everything will burn."

"So you murder children to prevent it?" Yuki spat.

"I use the resources available to me." Rahu drifted toward Rishi, ignoring Yuki completely now. "But this one... this one is different. I thought he might be simply a powerful incarnate, a tool I could shape. But the way the Prana responds to him, the way reality bends just from his presence..." His withered lips pulled into something like a smile. "You're more than an incarnate, aren't you, boy? You're something older. Something that shouldn't exist in a world with the Separation intact."

The pendant burned hotter. Rishi clutched it through his shirt, gasping. "I don't know what you're talking about—"

"No?" Rahu's voice dropped to a whisper. "Then let me show you."

He made a gesture, and the guards moved forward with a rod of black iron, its tip glowing with sickly light. They pressed it against the pendant, and Rishi screamed as the metal heated, seared his skin, burned through cloth and flesh—

The pendant cracked.

Not just cracked—shattered, the silver disk exploding into fragments that fell to the floor like dying stars.

And with its breaking, everything changed.

***

Pain and ecstasy merged into a single sensation as Rishi's body remembered what it truly was.

The manacles on his wrists exploded into vapor. A shockwave erupted from his body, throwing the guards backward like leaves in a storm. Only Yuki managed to keep her feet, bracing against the wall, her eyes wide with awe and terror.

Rishi convulsed, his back arching as power flooded through him—not just power but presence, each dimension bringing something newer with itself, an overwhelming sensation breaking and recreating his body with each passing moment.

His bones lengthened, snapping and reforming with wet cracks that should have killed him. Muscle wrapped around his frame, dense and powerful, erasing fifteen years of enforced frailty in seconds. His skin glowed with inner light, and when he opened his mouth to scream, what emerged was not a human sound but something vast and ancient—the first word spoken into void, the original breath that preceded all other creations.

I AM.

Memories flooded through him—not his memories, but older ones. Deeper ones. He remembered the first breath that became the universe. He remembered naming stars into existence. He remembered the wars between gods and demons, countless cycles, infinite iterations. He remembered dying and being born and dying again, the wheel turning, the Yuga spinning through eternity.

And he remembered the Immortal Sage—a brilliant, desperate mortal who had dared to break the pattern, who had looked at infinite suffering and said no more. The Separation had been an act of cosmic rebellion, a spit in the face of divine inevitability.

Rishi had respected that. Even now, even knowing what he was, he respected it.

When the transformation ended, he stood seven feet tall, his body corded with muscle, his eyes burning with light that had witnessed the birth of time itself. He turned to face Elder Rahu, and the floating head flinched.

"You're not what I expected," Rishi said, his voice resonant and terrible. "I can see you clearly now, Rahu. You're no elder. You're not even of this realm."

"I am ageless. I am—"

"You're a scavenger demon. A lesser spirit that survived the Separation by eating scraps of divinity." Rishi took a step forward, and Rahu retreated despite having no body to carry him. "You've been collecting incarnate children, haven't you? Trying to gather enough divine fragments to tear down the barrier. You want to flood this world with magic again so you can feast on the chaos."

Yuki inhaled sharply, betrayal flooding her features. "Elder... is this true?"

Rahu's composure shattered. "The Separation is failing anyway! The Sage's work was always temporary! I'm simply accelerating the inevitable—"

"By using children." Rishi's voice dropped to something cold and absolute. "By treating incarnations like weapons."

"They are weapons!" Rahu snarled, his fear transforming into desperate rage. "You most of all! You're the Beginning! The one who starts the wars that end worlds! I've watched cycle after cycle, seen the pattern repeat! Incarnations are the ones that head these wars! If I can control you, if I can guide your power, I can bring peace! I can reshape reality itself! No more suffering! No more cycles! I can—"

"Enough!"

Rishi felt it then—the architecture of the Citadel, woven from stolen divine power, anchored by artifacts soaked in Prana. He understood how fragile it all was. How easily it could be unmade.

The Immortal Sage had tried to stop the cycle by starving it. Perhaps it was time to try something different. Perhaps the memory of a golden age was stopping them from creating one.

"What are you doing?" Yuki's voice cracked. "My Lord—" She dropped to her knees, realizing she was in the presence of a true god, her instincts as an incarnate herself overwhelmed by his divine presence. "Please, stop—there are innocent people here—all of them are not evil—"

"I know." He met her eyes, and she saw the terrible sadness in them. "I'm sorry. I hate choosing the deserving, making that judgment, but I can't run away from my duty. I have to pass judgment."

He knelt and picked up the shattered fragments of his grandmother's pendant from the floor. The pieces were warm in his palm, and nostalgia flooded through him—memories of his mother's rough hands placing it around his neck, of her harsh words meant to keep him weak, to keep him alive, to keep him hers for just a little while longer.

The pendant hadn't been strong enough to hold him forever. But it had held him long enough.

He poured everything into the fragments—all the Prana saturating the air, all the divine power flowing through his awakened body, all the vast crushing weight of being the Beginning. The artifacts throughout the Citadel began to scream, overloading, cracking, bleeding light.

"NO!" Rahu shrieked, trying to flee, but there was nowhere to go.

The Citadel shattered like glass.

***

When it was over, when the fortress beyond space had collapsed back into void, Rishi stood in the ruins and felt himself dissolving, coming back to his human form, and slowly disintegrating from existence. His last conscience was to bring all the innocent incarnated out of the pocket universe that was The Citadel.

The story of this incarnation was toward making this world independent of magic—to see how far humanity could go. To realize the Immortal Sage Shukracharya's dream.

His mortal frame couldn't contain what he'd become, couldn't hold the Beginning once fully awakened. Like always, like every cycle, incarnation led to death.

But this time was different. This time, he'd chosen it.

The Separation will fail eventually, he thought as his body began to fade into light. Magic will return. The Yuga will turn. But not today. Not by Rahu's design. The Sage earned that much.

Far away, in a shop in the capital, Maya Vosh stopped sorting scrap and looked up at the empty sky. She didn't know how she knew, but she did: her son was gone. Her fragile, impossible son who had never really been hers at all.

She found herself smiling through tears. He'd survived. In his own way, he'd survived everything—her cruelty, the Kindred, even being a god.

"My good boy," she whispered to the smoke. "My dear boy."

She regretted not telling him when he was there with her. The words she should have said.

***

The Beginning scattered into light and memory, and the world exhaled. Maybe the wheel of Yuga still turned, but this one wouldn't be forced into destruction. Dhūma would forget that existing magic, and be left to start anew, independent of divine intervention. They would have the chance to create their own golden age.

Somewhere in the space between spaces, a consciousness that had witnessed the birth of time drifted toward the next incarnation, the next cycle, the next attempt to understand what it meant to be.

And in the ruins of the Citadel and sleeping bodies, Yuki stood alone, one of the survivors, and wondered if the Sage's rebellion had been doomed from the start—or if perhaps, just perhaps, this strange sickly boy who became a god for a few moments had found an option that progressed the Sage’s dream.

After the death of Rahu, she was free too—free to make a choice.

The Yuga would turn. But not today.

In this stagnation, they still had a choice, a choice to be more than just a battlefield between gods and demons. And Yuki was given that choice, like all the others in her world.

She knelt in the rubble and picked up a fragment of silver—a piece of the pendant that had protected Rishi, that had held him back just long enough for him to learn love, to understand sacrifice, to become something more than just The Beginning.

Above the dying world, the starless sky remained empty.

For now, that was enough.

Epilogue: A world without Magic

Yuki stood at the edge of the industrial wasteland, watching smoke curl toward an empty sky. In her hand, she held a fragment of silver—recovered from the Citadel's ruins, still warm to the touch, the last piece of Rishi's mortal life. The symbols etched into its surface no longer shifted. Whatever power it had held was spent.

She'd find Maya Vosh. Return it to her. Tell her what her son had done, what he'd sacrificed, what he'd chosen.

And then? Yuki didn't know. The Kindred was destroyed. Rahu was dead. Her whole life's purpose had been built on lies.

But as she started walking toward the scrap shop, she found herself smiling slightly. Rishi had taught her something in those final moments: that even gods could choose something other than their nature. That destiny was a wheel, but wheels could be slowed.

The Beginning would return. Someday, somewhere, in some part of the world, a child would be born who carried the First Breath. The cycle would turn again.

But what kind of incarnation would it be? Would that child be kind, like Rishi, choosing sacrifice over destiny? Or would it be merciless, indifferent to mortal suffering as so many had been before? Would humanity once again find itself at the mercy of forces beyond comprehension, helpless before the whims of the divine?

Yuki didn't know. No one could know.

But perhaps—perhaps that was exactly why they needed to build something worth preserving. Not for the gods, but for themselves.

But thanks to one sick, stubborn boy who'd learned to love his cruel mother and pity his tormentor, the universe had earned a reprieve.

It was a small victory against eternity.

But it was still a victory.

***

When Yuki finally stepped outside, night was falling—or what passed for night in Dhūma. But something was different. The sky, usually an unbroken slate of gray, now held color. Faint bands of orange and purple lingered where the sun had set, painting the horizon with hues she'd only read about in ancient texts.

Were these what they called sunsets?

She stood transfixed, watching the colors fade slowly into darkness. What had the boy chosen? Yuki asked herself again.

A speck of ash landed on her white sleeve, leaving a dark smudge. Then another. And another. She looked down at her clothes—once pristine, now soiled with the same grime that covered everything in the city. The divine artifacts that had kept her clean, that had marked her as separate from the dying world, were no longer working.

Not just death. Not just sacrifice. He'd chosen to let the world continue without divine intervention, to give humanity the chance to stumble forward on their own terms

The Separation was truly complete now. Magic had retreated entirely, taking with it even the small privileges she'd enjoyed as the Kindred's Recruiter.
